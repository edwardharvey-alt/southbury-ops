# Hearth — Strategy

**Capture and graduate: a vendor-first demand engine for independent food businesses**

**Date:** July 2026
**Author:** Ed Harvey
**For:** Robin Brian
**Status:** Proposal for co-founder review

---

## 1. The short version

Hearth has been defining itself by its **mechanic** — the drop — rather than its **mission**: helping independent food businesses own their customers and escape aggregator economics.

The mission is large. The mechanic is narrow. Building the business around the mechanic made the addressable market far smaller than it needed to be, and made every vendor conversation open with the hardest possible ask: *change how you operate*.

The proposal:

> **Stop asking vendors to change how they operate. Capture their customers wherever they already sell — for free — and let the data show them the drop.**

The drop is not abandoned. It becomes the **destination** rather than the entry ticket.

Three consequences:

1. **The net widens.** Any food business can join without changing anything. The behaviour-change gate moves from the front door to much further down the funnel.
2. **The sell inverts.** We stop persuading vendors that drops will work and start *showing* them: *140 of your customers live in Broadstone. That's a Friday drop — and you don't need anyone's permission to run it.* The data does the selling.
3. **The commercial model sharpens.** Capture is free. We earn only on demand we created. That is a one-sentence position no aggregator can copy and no ordering platform can claim.

**What does not change:** the drop model and its non-negotiables, the vendor-first architecture, honest capacity, no customer surcharge, and the fact that Hearth only grows when vendors do.

---

## 2. Why

### 2.1 The gate was wrong — not the market

We have been telling ourselves the market is small. It isn't. **The number we have been anchoring on is obsolete.**

The Canonical Manuscript states that a mature catchment supports 5–8 Hearth-grade businesses across ~225 UK catchments — implying a national ceiling of 1,125–1,800 vendors. **That figure was derived for a delivery drop model that needed route density and batching to make no-delivery-fee viable.** A café running collection drops and a food truck running pitch drops need none of that. The ceiling was a constraint of the *old* model, and the pivot removes it.

The real market:

- **50,235** takeaway and fast-food businesses in the UK, in a £24bn market with **low market share concentration**
- **63,047** active takeaway and sandwich shop establishments on the FSA register (March 2026)
- **~90,000** licensed food and drink premises in Great Britain
- **176,685** UK hospitality businesses, of which **97.7% are small**
- **~72%** of UK restaurants are independents, not chains
- **8,881** specialist cafés and coffee shops; **7,000+** street food vans

**TAM — independent UK food businesses with repeat, local, consumer demand: comfortably 100,000+.**

So the constraint was never the size of the market. It was **the gate we put in front of it** — asking every vendor to change how they operate before they had any evidence it would work. That gate, not the market, is what made Hearth feel small.

### 2.2 We were gating on the hardest thing

Every vendor conversation began by asking for a change in operating model *before* the vendor had any evidence it would work. That is the worst possible order:

- The vendor cannot see demand until they run a drop.
- They will not run a drop until they believe it will fill.
- They cannot believe it will fill until they see demand.

This is the vendor confidence gap — and we have been walking into it at the point of sale, with no data, no track record, and no scorecard to show.

### 2.3 The mechanic was never the moat

The uncomfortable finding from the competitive research: **"own your customers, escape aggregator commission" is not our insight.** It is the homepage copy of Flipdish, Slerp, ChowNow, Square, Storekit and GloriaFood. That category is crowded, well-funded, feature-rich — and one of its players is free.

What none of them have is **demand generation**. They are all *front doors*. They wait for customers to arrive. Not one of them knows where a vendor's customers live, knows the cricket club down the road, or tells the vendor what to run next.

That absence — not the drop — is our moat.

## 3. What Hearth is

**Mission.** Help independent food businesses build direct, lasting relationships with the people who eat their food — so that quality decides who thrives locally, not commission.

**Vision.** Every good local food business owns its customers. Every neighbourhood has food moments worth planning around.

**Positioning.**

> **Everyone else gives independents a better front door. Hearth brings people through it.**

**The bet — falsifiable, and the only one that matters.**

> A vendor will one day ask: *why use Hearth's ordering when Square's is free and better?*
>
> The only answer Hearth can ever give is: **because Hearth brings you customers Square never will.**
>
> If that stops being true, there is no business.

Everything in the build sequence exists either to make that question *reachable*, or to keep the answer *true*.

## 4. The competitive truth

Three layers. Only one is a real threat.

**Aggregators** — Just Eat, Deliveroo, Uber Eats. Not competitors. The antagonist; the thing we define against. We will never fight them for orders and should not try.

**Direct-ordering platforms** — Flipdish, Slerp, Square, ChowNow, Storekit, GloriaFood, Toast. The genuine threat. They already own our pitch. Better funded, more featured. **Our ordering will be worse than theirs for years, and that is acceptable — provided we never sell ordering.**

**The demand layer** — hosts, affinity communities, local moments, cadence coaching, and above all the intelligence that reads a vendor's own customer base. **Empty.** Hotplate is the nearest neighbour: no host layer, no delivery, US only, and it puts its own brand in front of the vendor's.

The instruction that follows is absolute:

> **Build down to be credible. Build up to be unbeatable.**
> Ordering is the price of entry. It is given away and never sold.
> The moment a Hearth sales conversation is about order pages, we have lost it.

## 5. The model: capture and graduate

The system is a loop. Every capability exists to turn it.

**Capture → Own → Read → Recommend → Activate → Gather → Capture more.**

1. **Capture.** Every way a vendor sells — the counter, the van, an ordering window, a drop — records a named, consented, contactable local person. So does every way they *don't* sell: a sold-out drop, a "tell me about the next one", an out-of-area postcode.
2. **Own.** All of it lands in one customer asset belonging to the vendor. Named, located, consented, reachable. **A customer who cannot be reached is not an asset.**
3. **Read.** Hearth clusters that asset geographically, tracks recency and repeat behaviour, monitors cadence, and knows which contexts sit near which clusters.
4. **Recommend.** Hearth does not report — it recommends. What to run, where, and when, in plain sentences from real data.
5. **Activate.** The right people, in the right voice, through the right channel. The vendor's own list. The host's group, in the host's words.
6. **Gather.** The moment happens. And it captures more customers — which widens the loop.

**The aggregator runs this same loop. Theirs compounds for the platform. Ours compounds for the vendor.** That inversion is the whole company.

### Why this fixes the funnel

- **The entry ask collapses** — from *"change how you operate"* to *"put a QR on your counter."* Modelled adoption moves from roughly 8–25% to 25–55%, depending on archetype.
- **The chicken-and-egg breaks.** Today a vendor must fill a drop before they have a list. Under capture-first, **the list exists before the drop does.**
- **Time-to-first-value collapses** — from weeks (brand, menu, Stripe, drop, promotion, hope) to an afternoon (name, page, QR). Time-to-first-value is the metric that decides early churn.
- **The data sells the drop.** We stop persuading and start evidencing.

## 6. The four demand contexts

Where does a drop's demand come from? There are exactly four answers, and they are not equal.

| Context | What it is | Whose demand | Rhythm |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Presence** | Where the vendor already trades — counter, van, stall | **Owned**, already existing | Continuous |
| **Host** | A place *and* an occasion — pub, club, school | **Borrowed** | Episodic |
| **Affinity community** | An audience *and* a shared context — gym, office, nursery | **Borrowed** | Continuous |
| **Neighbourhood** | Demand derived from the vendor's own customer asset | **Owned**, created by Hearth | Recurring |

### 6.1 The neighbourhood drop is the destination

**A host is borrowed demand. A neighbourhood drop is owned demand.** Hearth's entire thesis is that the vendor should own their demand. It follows that the well-informed neighbourhood drop is not a lesser cousin of the host event — it is **the purest expression of the model**, and the only one that proves the intelligence layer works.

> **Hosts and affinity communities are bridges, not destinations.**
>
> A vendor with no list borrows an audience. Every borrowed-audience drop captures those people into the vendor's *own* asset. Eventually the vendor has enough owned demand in that area to run without the bridge at all.

That is graduation — the same pattern as capture → drop, one level up.

**The caveat that keeps bridges permanently valuable:** some occasions never graduate away, because the *gathering itself* is the point. A cricket club after a match. A school fundraiser. Those are not scaffolding — they are genuine community moments, and they are worth running forever.

### 6.2 Ordering windows — identical mechanics, opposite intent

A vendor-set ordering window lets a vendor open ordering when it suits them and close it when it doesn't: a Saturday morning collection, a Thursday evening pre-order, a weekday lunch run.

**We should be honest that the mechanics are table stakes.** Flipdish, Slerp and Square all let a vendor set hours and pause ordering. We match them competently and we do not try to win there — that would be fighting below the stop line (§11).

What differs is not the feature. It is the intent behind it, and that shows up in three places.

**One — the default is closed.**
Every other platform is designed for always-on. Being closed is an exception: a greyed-out button, an "unavailable" message, a failure state to be minimised. **Hearth is designed the other way round.** Closed is the resting state, and it is dignified: the vendor's page isn't broken, it's simply telling you when they'll be back — and capturing you while it does. Open is the event.

**Two — the window has a real capacity, so nobody ever rejects an order.**

Aggregators need a reject button because they push unlimited demand at a finite kitchen — and then punish the vendor for using it. Just Eat contracts in some markets require a **minimum 95% order acceptance rate**, with restaurants taken offline automatically if they fail to acknowledge an order within five minutes. Healthy Habits described a Just Eat representative visiting in person to remove their ability to reject orders at all.

Hearth never over-commits the vendor in the first place. Capacity is declared up front; ordering closes when it is reached.

> **You don't reject orders on Hearth. You never accept more than you said you could make.**

The reject button is a symptom of a broken model, not a feature to be matched. (The vendor still needs an early close — *"we've run out"* — with clean refunds. That is a pause, not a rejection.)

**Three — the window is a promise, not a setting.**
For every other platform, opening hours are admin. Change them freely; nobody notices and nobody minds. For Hearth, the window is the thing customers **plan around** — which means consistency matters, and Hearth is the only platform that will say so:

> *"Your Saturday window has moved three times this month. Customers find it hard to plan around that."*

No other platform would ever say this, because no other platform cares whether a vendor is consistent — they only want more orders. **This is the cadence coaching of the moat, applied to windows.** It is also the sharpest illustration of the alignment: we are the only platform whose interests are served by telling a vendor to be *predictable* rather than *available*.

**And the invariant that keeps it honest: Hearth never sells "always on."** Every ordering surface is bounded — by presence (the vendor is there), or by time and capacity (the window, the drop). We will not offer an unbounded, anytime, anywhere remote order channel. That single rule is what keeps us out of a head-on fight with Square and Flipdish on their own ground, and it is what keeps the brand intact.

**Capacity within a window: ring-fenced, not dynamic.**

The real constraint in a shop is the shared kitchen — online orders and walk-ins compete for the same oven and the same hands. The tempting fix is a live dial: *tell us how busy you are, and online capacity flexes accordingly.* **We should not build it.**

- It **breaks the promise**. The window is what customers plan around. A number that moves because the vendor's morning got busy turns the window from a commitment into a weather report — and a customer who sees slots at 9am and none at 10am has experienced exactly the unreliability we exist to remove.
- It **corrupts honest scarcity**. Our capacity signal is trustworthy because it is declared in advance and respected absolutely. A number that moves for reasons the customer cannot see is, experientially, indistinguishable from manufactured scarcity — however honest the intent.
- It **won't be used when it's needed**. The vendor is slammed. That is precisely when they will not stop to move a slider. The dial would be stale exactly when it matters.
- It is **dangerous on the money path**. Mutable capacity while orders are in flight multiplies a class of bug we have already fought once.

**Instead:**

- **Ring-fence the capacity.** A declared block — *20 collection slots on Saturday* — genuinely committed, not competing with the counter. This is the drop model applied to a window, and it is the whole point of pre-selling: **pre-sold work should be the predictable part of the day, not the part that flexes with walk-in chaos.**
- **Slot it.** Four orders per fifteen-minute collection slot, rather than twenty sprayed across three hours. Slotting is the real answer to kitchen contention — it paces online orders to a rate the kitchen can absorb alongside walk-ins, without ever changing the number after publication.
- **Keep the pause binary.** One tap: *"we've run out."* Honest, legible, no corrupted number.
- **Planned variation is fine.** *Saturdays: 20 slots. Bank holiday: 10.* Set before ordering opens, so the customer never sees it change.

> **Hearth's job is to make demand predictable — not to make supply reactive.**

A live capacity dial is a vendor absorbing chaos in real time. Ring-fenced, slotted capacity is a vendor *removing* it in advance. The first is a coping mechanism; the second is the product.

**Where "how busy am I" does belong: the intelligence layer, as an input, not a control.** Over time Hearth notices the pattern and says so — *"Your Saturday window overlaps your busiest counter hours. Vendors usually move it later, or slot it."* That is coaching, not a dial. It sits in the moat rather than below the stop line, and it is the version of the idea that only Hearth could build.

### 6.3 Hosts — a place and an occasion

**The unit is not the venue. It is the person.** A host is not a pub; it is *a landlord with a WhatsApp group and a reason to send a message*. No motivated individual, no host — however good the venue.

**The host qualifying gate**, mirroring the vendor gate:

1. A **reachable audience** — a group they can actually message, not merely footfall.
2. A **recurring reason to gather** — fixtures, Friday nights, term dates.
3. **A person who will actually send the message.**

Fail any one and it is an event, not a host.

| Host archetype | What they actually want | Cadence | Friction |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pub / bar** | Wet sales and dwell time. Food serves the drinks. | Weekly. **Best archetype.** | Low — one decision-maker |
| **Sports club** | Member experience and fundraising | Fixture-led, seasonal | Volunteer-run, slow decisions |
| **School / PTA** | Money for the school | A few events a year | High — permissions, safeguarding. Huge WhatsApp reach |
| **Residential / street group** | A good local thing | Ad hoc | The natural bridge to neighbourhood drops — a street WhatsApp group *is* a host |

**The pub is the only host with a direct commercial motive that does not depend on our revenue share** — food sells drinks. That makes it the easiest sell and the highest cadence. **If we test one host archetype, test a pub.**

### 6.4 Affinity communities — an audience and a shared context

A gym is not a host. Nobody gathers; there is no venue, no occasion, no service window. What a gym has is **an audience with a shared context** — and that context makes a curated menu genuinely relevant rather than a gimmick.

*Healthy Habits × a local gym* is the obvious first instance, and it is almost too neat: a healthy-food cafe serving a macro-conscious audience with a curated standing collection menu.

**Why affinity may be the stronger archetype:**

- **Continuous, not episodic.** A pub night is an event. A gym is a standing Wednesday. That is cadence — the precondition for habit formation — handed to us for free.
- **Menu affinity is real value.** A protein-forward, macro-labelled selection is a genuinely better product for that audience. A pub cannot offer that.
- **No venue, no logistics, no service window.** Materially lower friction than a host.
- **It is a replicable template.** *Gym × healthy vendor* is a **pattern** that can be run in every catchment. So is *office × lunch vendor*, and *nursery × family meals*. **Hosts are bespoke. Affinities are repeatable** — and repeatability is exactly what a geographic rollout business needs.

| Affinity archetype | The shared context | Cadence |
|---|---|---|
| **Gym / fitness studio** | Health, macros, post-workout | Weekly, standing |
| **Workplace / office** | Weekday lunch, team ordering | Weekday, predictable. **Underrated** |
| **Nursery / school gate** | Family meals, time-poor parents | Weekly |
| **Co-working space** | Lunch, community | Weekday |

**Ask the killer question first.** A pub sells drinks, so food serves its own P&L. **A gym gets what?** Many gyms already run a café, shake bar or supplement line — in which case we are *cannibalising* their revenue, not adding to it. Establish this before anything else.

**Pay affinity partners in early access, not margin.**

> *"Gym members get first choice on Wednesday's menu, 24 hours before anyone else."*

No discount. No margin erosion. No payout infrastructure. It is exactly the insider mechanic already specced in the Drop Communications Architecture, it gives the partner a genuine member benefit that costs nothing, and it keeps discounting — off-brand for Hearth — entirely out of the model.

**Keep the curated menu a subset, not a second kitchen.** A separate line means separate prep and separate SKUs — real cost to a small cafe. It should be a *relabelled selection* of what the vendor already makes, presented for that audience.

### 6.5 The chicken-and-egg dissolves

The concern is real, but the asymmetry resolves it. **Which side is scarce?**

- **Vendors:** 5–8 per catchment. Expensive to acquire. Hard to convince. **Scarce.**
- **Hosts and affinity partners:** every pub wants food that sells drinks; every club wants member service and fundraising; every school wants money; every gym wants a member perk. **Abundant, cheap, eager.**

Therefore: **we never build a two-sided marketplace.** We recruit hosts and affinity partners **on demand, per vendor, by hand.** Rob is precisely the instrument — it is local relationship work, not platform work, and it works fine at our scale.

The host directory, discovery and matching layer only earns its keep once there is vendor density in a catchment. That is a later phase, not the next one.

## 7. Menu intelligence — advising without presuming

Vendors do not need to be told what to cook. Telling a proud independent what to put on a plate is the fastest way to lose them — and they are precisely the people Hearth selects for.

But there is a great deal Hearth can honestly say, and it sits on one clean line:

> **Hearth advises on the occasion, the shape, and the audience. Never on the food.**
>
> A chef does not need help cooking. They need to know *who is buying, and when*. The vendor owns the food. Always.

"Family supper" is not a recipe. It is a **demand observation** — *Friday evening drops in residential areas skew to family-sized orders*. What a family supper looks like in that kitchen is entirely the vendor's business.

### 7.1 What Hearth can legitimately say

**Their own data, reflected back.** The safest and most trusted layer, and nobody else has it. *Your three-item drops fill faster than your five-item drops. The family bundle sold out every time it appeared. One item is 40% of your orders — it's your anchor.* This is observation, not advice: the vendor's own business, told back to them clearly.

**Menu shape and operational structure.** The genuine sweet spot, and under-exploited. Hearth can advise on the *architecture* of a menu without ever touching the food:

- **Menu size.** Smaller menus fill faster, execute better, and protect quality — this is already a locked principle in the Canonical Manuscript.
- **Capacity mix.** Which item is the capacity driver, and which items are free-riding on it.
- **Bundle structure.** Whether a family bundle exists, and whether it is priced to be chosen.
- **Basket size.** Larger baskets materially improve the vendor's economics, because payment costs carry a fixed component (§9.2). This is a real, honest, financial observation.

**Occasion and audience framing.** Not culinary. *This drop lands on a Friday in a residential postcode — expect family-sized orders. This one is a weekday gym collection — expect single portions.* Hearth names the occasion. The vendor decides what meets it.

**Cross-vendor patterns — later, and only when true.** *Across Hearth, drops with a family bundle fill faster.* This is the collective intelligence promise already planted in our landing page copy, and it is a genuine network effect. But it is **data-gated**. With one vendor there is no cross-vendor pattern, and inventing one would be a brand violation. **Do not write this copy until there is real data to write it against.**

### 7.2 Inspiration is editorial, never algorithmic

There is a difference between **intelligence** (derived from data; must be honest; must degrade gracefully) and **inspiration** (editorial; curated; human).

Seasonal prompts, occasion ideas, a note on what independents around Hearth are running this month — these are legitimate and very much in Hearth's editorial voice. But they are **written, not computed**. No "trending." No algorithmic language. No AI-generated suggestions presented as insight.

### 7.3 The danger to name

**Menu intelligence can quietly become homogenisation.**

If Hearth tells every vendor that family bundles fill faster, every vendor runs family bundles — and Hearth becomes an algorithm flattening local food into a single optimised shape. That is the aggregator, wearing our clothes.

The guardrail: **Hearth advises on shape and occasion. The food stays theirs.** If we ever find ourselves recommending dishes, we have crossed the line that defines us.

## 8. The capability map

Eight domains, three tiers. The tiers are not a maturity model — they are a competitive statement.

**Tier 1 — Foundation (table stakes).**
Identity & Offer · Demand Capture · Moment Design & Fulfilment.
Everyone has these. Be competent. Never be distinctive here.

**Tier 2 — The Asset (contested).**
Customer Asset & Consent · Activation & Communications.
Flipdish, Slerp, Square and ChowNow all claim this ground, loudly.

**Tier 3 — The Moat (empty).**
Intelligence & Coaching · Community, Host & Affinity Network.
**No competitor has these. Hearth lives or dies here.**

**Cross-cutting — Trust & Governance.**
Honest scarcity backed by real capacity. No manufactured urgency. No customer surcharge. No hidden payment margin. Server-side pricing authority. Not a tier — a constraint on every tier. And quietly a differentiator: each of these is something a competitor *could* do, and we won't.

**The uncomfortable part:** the two capabilities that constitute our entire differentiation are the two we have built least. Hosts exist as entities with no network. Intelligence is the critical gap. That is the natural order — the floor had to come first — but it tells us exactly where the next year goes, and it is not more ordering surfaces.

## 9. The commercial model

### 9.1 The principle

> **Hearth only earns on demand it created.**
> Not on transactions it merely processed.

| Moment | Fee | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Capture (any door) | **Free, forever** | It is the wedge. Not a trial, not a teaser. |
| Counter / till sale | **Nothing. We don't touch it.** | The vendor already had that customer. |
| Vendor's own ordering window | **None or minimal** | Their own audience, arriving on their own. |
| **Drop** | **10%** | The sale would not have existed without Hearth. |
| **Host-led drop** | **10% + host share** | Hearth brought the community. |
| **Affinity drop** | **10%** | Hearth brought the audience. Partner paid in early access, not margin. |
| **Re-activation of a dormant customer** | **10%** | Hearth found them and brought them back. |

**The sentence a vendor hears:**

> *"Building your customer base is free. We only earn when we bring you a sale you wouldn't have had."*

### 9.2 Payments — the honest position

We investigated whether routing payments through Hearth could be *cheaper* for vendors than their existing card machine. **It cannot, and we should stop hoping it can.**

- A QR order is card-not-present. Stripe charges **1.5% + 20p**. A SumUp reader charges **1.69% flat, no fixed fee**. The 20p is decisive: **Stripe is more expensive than their card machine on every basket below roughly £105.**
- Stripe's Pay by Bank, which looked like the escape route, is **1.5% + 30p** — *worse than Stripe's own card rate*. Stripe keeps the open-banking saving rather than passing it on. It does work inside Connect Express (a capability flag, not a rebuild) and it eliminates chargebacks — but it is not a cost win.
- The genuine saving (flat pennies per transaction) requires a **direct open-banking provider outside Stripe Connect**, which means owning payout splits, refunds and compliance ourselves. That is a year-two-or-three project at volume. Not now.

**Three reasons this is not existential:**

1. **On a drop, the payment cost is compared against nothing.** Without Hearth, that £30 order does not exist. We pay ~2.2% to *create* a sale — against aggregators taking 20–30% of sales the vendor already had.
2. **Every competitor pays the same rates.** Card economics are the cost of online ordering for the entire category. We are not disadvantaged relative to anyone we compete with.
3. **Payment cost is not in Hearth's P&L.** It is a vendor cost line. It has no effect on our revenue model or our viability.

**What it did do is sharpen the model.** There is no payment-cost win available to offset routing counter sales through Hearth — which is precisely why **the till QR must be capture-only**. That is not a compromise forced on us by Stripe's pricing. It is a principled boundary, and it makes the fee model cleaner.

### 9.3 The free-rider question — and why we don't charge for windows

There is a structural flaw in "free capture, 10% on drops": **the free layer carries all our cost and none of our revenue.**

But it is important to locate the risk precisely, because it is *not* where it first appears.

**The pure capture vendor is not the problem.** A vendor with a till QR building a list costs us very little and is doing exactly what we want — proving they have customers we can connect with in new ways. That is the on-ramp working.

**The real free-rider is the window and table-QR vendor.** They use Hearth heavily. They take real orders. They escape the aggregators, keep more margin, and consume genuine support — and they pay us **nothing**. Worse, they may feel no need to move to drops at all, because they already have what they wanted. If they never graduate, we are a free Slerp for them.

#### The tempting fix — a 5% fee on window orders — is wrong

It *feels* fair, because we instinctively compare against a 25–30% aggregator. But **the relevant competitor for a window order is not Just Eat. It is Square.**

A vendor choosing where to run their Saturday collection window is choosing between Hearth and Square Online, Flipdish or GloriaFood — **which charge nothing for exactly that**. Charging 5% would mean charging a *premium* for the one capability where we are demonstrably worse than free competitors. A rational vendor does that arithmetic in ten seconds and leaves. It is fighting below the stop line, at a price disadvantage, on ordering — precisely what §4 forbids.

It also breaks the cleanest asset we have. *"We only earn when we bring you a sale you wouldn't have had"* is a sentence a vendor trusts immediately. *"…and also 5% of your own regulars"* is not.

#### The answer is selection, not pricing

If a vendor has no intention of ever running a drop, **they are not a Hearth vendor.** The soft start is not a free ordering platform — it is a **de-risked on-ramp to drops**, and someone who wants the on-ramp without the destination should be told so, plainly, at qualification. This becomes an explicit gate (§10.1).

That is selectivity worn with confidence. It costs nothing, it is entirely on-brand, and it converts a pricing problem into a **qualification** problem — which we can actually solve, because we hand-onboard one or two vendors a month and the filter is us.

#### Two backstops

**Graduation becomes the intelligence layer's explicit job.** Not passive reporting — active pressure. *"You now have 140 customers in Broadstone. That's a drop."* Repeated, patiently, until they run one. **This reframes Insights one final time: it is not a dashboard, not merely coaching — it is the mechanism that converts our free tier.** It is the strongest argument yet for it being the primary post-stop-line build.

**And if some still don't graduate: a platform subscription, not a transaction fee.** A heavy window user who never runs a drop is receiving genuine platform value. Charge them for **the platform** — not for a slice of their own customers' orders. That preserves the principle exactly, it is ChowNow's model, and it avoids a per-order price fight we would lose.

#### What we accept

Some window vendors will free-ride. At roughly £150 a year in cost-to-serve, **that is an acceptable acquisition cost** — they are a local reference, they feed the catchment demand map, and some will graduate late. It only becomes a problem if selection is sloppy and the number grows.

#### And two ideas that stay rejected

**An activation fee at signup.** It would destroy the pivot — *"put a QR on your counter, it's free"* becoming *"…that'll be £150"* reinstates the exact barrier we just removed — and it asks for money before we have delivered anything.

**Charging a vendor to reach their own customers.** Tempting, recurring, and *precisely* the lock-in mechanism our own evidence file cites against DoorDash. **The customer asset is theirs: exportable, contactable, unconditionally.** If we ever gate it, we have become the thing we exist to oppose.

**Permanently off the table:** customer surcharges, hidden payment margins, and discounting. All three are competitive disadvantages accepted on purpose.

## 10. Who Hearth is for

### 10.1 The vendor gate

There are now **two** tests, and a vendor must pass both.

**Test one — demand.** *Does this vendor have repeat, local, consumer demand?*
(This replaces the old gate, *"is this vendor shaped like a drop?"*)

**Test two — intent.** *Do they intend, eventually, to run drops?*

The second test is new, and it is the answer to the free-rider question (§9.3). The soft start is **a de-risked on-ramp to drops** — not a free ordering platform. A vendor who wants the on-ramp without ever reaching the destination is not a Hearth vendor, and we should say so at qualification, in plain words:

> *"The QR is how we get you to drops without asking you to take it on faith. If you never intend to run one, Hearth isn't the right fit — go and use Square. It's free, and it's good."*

That is selectivity worn with confidence. It costs nothing, it builds trust, and it is a far better filter than any fee. Sending a poor-fit vendor to a competitor, cheerfully, is one of the strongest signals of confidence we can give.

**The intent test does not mean demanding a commitment.** We are not asking a vendor to promise a date, or to change how they operate on day one — that is the very thing the pivot removes. We are asking whether the *destination* appeals. A vendor who says *"yes, if you can show me it will work"* passes. A vendor who says *"I just want a free ordering page"* does not.

| Archetype | Verdict |
|---|---|
| **Shop-front makers** — bakery, deli, cafe (Healthy Habits) | **Yes.** Repeat, local, high-frequency. Data-rich. |
| **Street food / food trucks** | **Yes.** Repeat and local. Weakest owned list today — so capture is the wedge, drops come later. |
| **Controlled restaurants** | **Yes, but hardest.** Real behaviour change, and the archetype Flipdish and Slerp fight hardest for. |
| **Pop-up / supper club chefs** | **Yes, but thin.** Natively drop-shaped, but small and hard to find. |
| **Caterers** | **No.** Their customer is a wedding, a funeral, a corporate away-day — one-off, often out-of-area, often on someone else's budget. **They fail the repeat-local test, and capture builds nothing that compounds.** Possibly a peripheral revenue line one day. Never a strategic entry point. |

### 10.2 Archetype configuration

Yes — but **configure the surface, never the spine.**

A truck's drop defaults to collect-now and van-sized capacity. A bakery's defaults to Saturday collection slots. A gym partnership defaults to a standing weekly menu. Same object, different presets — so the platform feels built for them.

But every archetype gets the *same* customer asset, the *same* intelligence, the *same* host and affinity network. **If archetype configuration ever means "food trucks get a different product," we have split the build and lost the compounding** — and the whole point is that a truck's customers and a bakery's customers feed the same demand map of the same catchment.

### 10.3 The onboarding unlock

Archetype-configured onboarding is right. But the bigger prize is this:

> **Onboard the vendor with a partnership already lined up.**

Not *"here's your page, go find demand."* Instead: *here's your page, here's your QR — and here's a gym with 400 members who want a Wednesday collection.*

That is a vendor with demand on **day one**, before they have done anything. It collapses the cold start. It answers vendor confidence **at the point of sale** — the exact moment we currently have no answer. And it is work Rob is perfectly placed to do, because it is local relationship work, not platform work.

**But do not build an archetype-configured onboarding system yet.** Get Healthy Habits live, run the discovery conversations, and line up **one affinity partner for HH by hand**. If that partnership works, we will know exactly what to configure — and we will have proved the most valuable pattern in the model.

## 11. What we build, in what order

### Standing rules

1. **Nothing here delays Healthy Habits go-live.** HH ships on the current drop model. It is the evidence engine and it comes first. Every week it slips is a week we build on assumptions instead of data.
2. **The capture layer is table stakes. Build it cheap, build it fast, then stop.**
3. **Audit-first, always.** No new mode is added on top of known drift.

### Phase 0 — Audit (blocking)

- **Coherence Audit Pass A.** `drop_type`, `audience_scope` and timing re-derivation already drift across the picker, `create-drop` and `update-drop`. The new ordering modes are new values of exactly those fields. **Adding modes on top of drift produces silent failures on the money path.**
- **Confirm the true state of T5-8 (interest registration).** Believed shipped, but "shipped" is ambiguous and the difference matters. Does it work when *no drop is live*? Does it capture consent and a contact channel? Does it write to the customer asset, or a side table? If it only works against an open drop, it cannot serve as a follow-the-vendor door.

### Phase 1 — The anchor

- **The permanent vendor page.** *The unlock — and it is not currently in the backlog.* Everything in Hearth is drop-scoped, and drop links expire. **A QR sticker on a table or a van cannot point at a drop URL.** Stickers last months; drops last days. We need a durable address — `lovehearth.co.uk/{vendor}` — that always resolves to whatever is true now: ordering open, drop announced, drop live, or nothing on. **The "nothing on" state is not an empty state — it is a capture surface**, and it is the state the page will be in most of the time.
- **Vendor QR vs drop QR.** Two distinct artefacts, clearly labelled. The durable QR points at the page. The drop QR points at a drop and is short-lived by design.

*Healthy Habits has already asked, unprompted, for a permanent QR code. This is the customer telling us what to build.*

### Phase 2 — Capture doors

Mostly **configuration of the drop object we already have**, not new objects. That is why this is cheaper than it looks.

- **Table QR / order-ahead** — order and pay. Justified where the vendor saves staff time or the customer skips a queue.
- **Till QR — capture only. No ordering, no payment.** *"Scan to hear what's next."*
- **Vendor-set ordering windows** — a drop with a long window, large capacity, and recurrence (§6.2). Still bounded. Still closes. **Hearth never sells "always on."**
- **Follow / notify-me** — capture without a sale. Probably the highest-leverage door we have, and the one we have most under-rated.
- **Sold-out capture** — currently our most wasted moment. Someone arrives at a full drop, demonstrates real demand, and we learn nothing.
- **Bring your own list** — import. A data-rich vendor starts with a base, not zero.

### Phase 3 — Asset integrity (ships *with* Phase 2, not after)

- **Identity resolution.** A customer who orders at the counter, then at a drop, then through a window must become **one** customer, not three. Without this, *"every way you sell builds one customer base"* is a claim we cannot honour, the repeat-customer signal silently fails, and the intelligence layer reads three strangers where there is one regular.
- **Capture origin on every touch.** Which surface, which place, which host or partner, when. **This cannot be retro-fitted.** The data is captured from the first order, or it never exists.

### ⛔ Phase 4 — The stop line

At this point Hearth is *a competent ordering platform with a good customer list.*

**So is Flipdish. So is Slerp. So is Square. One of them is free.**

Everything above this line is table stakes — the price of entry, not the business. **If we stop here, Hearth is a worse-funded Slerp, and it loses.**

**Every hour beyond this line belongs to the moat.** No new capture surfaces. No feature-parity chasing. If a vendor asks for something Flipdish already does well, the answer is *"not yet"* — unless it feeds the customer asset or the intelligence layer.

### Phase 5 — Intelligence (the primary moat investment)

The asymmetry is decisive:

- **The intelligence layer needs nobody.** It works for vendor number one, on day one, from their own data. No network, no counterparty, no cold start. **It is unilateral.**
- **A host network is two-sided.** It needs vendors *and* hosts before it is worth anything to either.

For a part-time founder with one live vendor, building the two-sided thing first is close to the worst available move. **Intelligence is the safer moat — and it is the thing that lets vendor number twenty sell themselves, which is our actual bottleneck.**

- **Geographic clustering.** The foundational primitive; produces the single most important sentence Hearth can say. Must **degrade gracefully** — with eleven customers it says *"not enough yet."* A fabricated demand signal is a brand violation.
- **The recommendation surface.** Insights is currently framed as a dashboard. It should not be. A dashboard reports what happened; the vendor still has to work out what to do. **Closing that gap is our differentiation.** Sentences, not charts.
- **Cadence monitoring and the scorecard.** Coaching through the first ten drops. The copy is already written in the Repetition Layer Voice Spec.
- **Menu intelligence** (§7) — occasion, shape and audience. Never the food.
- **The neighbourhood recommendation.** The destination of the whole model: *you no longer need the cricket club — you have 140 customers in Broadstone. Run it yourself.*

### Phase 6 — Host and affinity, as concierge (in parallel; no build)

**Not a platform. A motion.**

- Recruit hosts and affinity partners **by hand, per vendor.** Rob leads.
- Host onboarding stays near-zero: name, place, audience size and channel, the windows they want filled. A surface to share the drop **in their own voice**, and see what happened.
- **Pay hosts manually until it hurts.** At one or two hosts, a bank transfer and an honest statement is fine — and it buys us a year. The revenue-share build (bank details, payouts, a second Connect account type) is deferred until volume forces it.
- Affinity partners are paid in **early access, not margin**.

### Phase 7 — The network (later, and only with density)

Host and affinity **directory, discovery and matching**. *This host has a Friday window and 200 members; this vendor has 83 customers within two miles and capacity for 40.* No platform in the UK or US can say that sentence.

**But it only earns its keep with vendor density in a catchment.** Building a two-sided network for one vendor wastes the only scarce resource we have — build time.

## 12. The ambition — and how we get there

### 12.1 The market ladder

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| **TAM** | Independent UK food businesses with repeat, local, consumer demand | **~100,000+** |
| **SAM** | Quality-led, control-caring, would genuinely want drops — our selectivity applied | **~10,000–15,000** |
| **SOM** | Realistic reach over a decade | **1,000–3,000 vendors** |

At current unit economics that is **£10m–£30m of annual revenue**. This is not a lifestyle business, and we should stop modelling it as one.

### 12.2 Depth beats breadth — the lever we have not been pulling

We keep reaching for *more vendors*. The larger lever is **revenue per vendor** — and the pivot has just handed it to us.

Under drop-only, a vendor ran one, maybe two drops a week. Under the four demand contexts (§6), a mature vendor with a rich customer asset can run **several moments a week**: a Wednesday gym affinity drop, a Friday neighbourhood drop, a Saturday pub host drop — with windows and presence capture running underneath. Same vendor. Same acquisition cost. **Three to four times the revenue.**

| | GMV per drop | Moments per week | Revenue to Hearth |
|---|---|---|---|
| Our old modelling | £900 | 2.5 | **£10.4k / vendor / yr** |
| Deepened | £1,100 | 4 | **£20.2k / vendor / yr** |

> **Aim higher by going deeper, not wider.**
> Fifty vendors we know well, each running four moments a week, is a **£1m business** — and a far more achievable one at our throughput than five hundred vendors running one.

It is also, conveniently, exactly the business our strategy already says we want: selectivity, depth, cadence, community. **The ambitious version and the on-brand version are the same version.**

Depth also comes first in sequence, because it works with the vendors we already have. Distribution does not.

### 12.3 The growth engine — how we break the throughput ceiling

Two part-time people can onboard one to two vendors a month. **No platform scales like that, and we should stop planning as though it might.**

But the ceiling is not a sales problem. **It is a product problem.** Every hour a human spends onboarding, persuading or coaching is an hour the product has not yet automated. And there is a convergence worth naming plainly:

> **The intelligence layer is simultaneously our moat, our salesperson, our coach, and our retention mechanism.**
> **Building the moat *is* the scaling solution. There is no trade-off between the two — they are the same work.**

**The test that keeps us honest:**

> **If founder-led acquisition is still the majority of our vendors in year three, we have a job, not a company.**

Every engine below exists to shrink that share.

---

#### The plan: four engines

**Engine 1 · Productise the coach** — *first, and it gates everything else.*

Human coaching through the first ten drops is currently what stops vendors churning. The Repetition Layer Voice Spec has **already written this copy** — the scorecard variants, the cadence-drift line, the "what's normal at drop three" reassurance. Encoding it removes our largest recurring human cost.

**This must land before self-serve onboarding.** Onboard vendors at volume before the coach is encoded, and they churn in the fragile weeks with nobody holding them. That is the one sequencing error that would actively hurt us.

**Engine 2 · Self-serve stage one**

Claim a page, print a QR — no human required. Hotplate proves zero-touch onboarding is achievable even for drops. This removes setup labour entirely, and it is only possible *because* of the pivot: there is nothing to set up until money moves.

**Engine 3 · The intelligence layer sells the drop**

Today Ed or Robin must *persuade*. Tomorrow the vendor's own data does it: *"140 of your customers live in Broadstone."* **This is the only mechanism that lets vendor number twenty convert with no founder in the room** — and it is the same build as the moat.

*After engines 1–3, throughput is bounded by awareness rather than by founder hours.*

**Engine 4 · Vendor referral — the only compounding channel**

This is currently **absent from every document we have**, and it is the one that turns linear growth into a curve.

Self-serve, operators and partnerships each add a *fixed* number of vendors a year. **Referral scales with the base we already have.** Independents know each other; a warm introduction from a peer beats any cold approach, and it costs nothing. Above roughly 0.8 referrals per vendor per year, with churn contained, it begins to run on its own.

**The reward must be status and early access — never a discount.** Discounting is off-brand and erodes the vendor's margin to buy growth we should be earning. *"You brought someone in — you get first look at what we build next"* is the Hearth-shaped version.

**This needs a mechanic, and we don't have one.** It should be designed.

---

#### Later optionality — not the plan

These are real, and worth protecting the architectural paths for. They are **not** what year one and two rest on, and we should not let them distract from engines 1–4.

- **Catchment operators on revenue share.** The natural endgame for a geographic business — local operators run their own patch. But it **requires self-serve to exist first**, otherwise every operator is simply another Ed. Robin is the prototype. A year-three-plus conversation.
- **Chains and trade bodies.** A pub company, a gym chain, a street-food association: one conversation yielding many partners. Lumpy, potentially large, and the affinity template (§6.4) is what makes it repeatable. Worth a conversation when we have something proven to bring them.
- **Demand-led acquisition.** Recruit the host or affinity partner *first*, then approach a vendor with something they cannot refuse: *"There is a gym with 400 members who want a Wednesday collection."* Easier than *"join my platform"*, and work Rob can lead — but it is a **tactic within** the engines above, not an engine in itself.

---

#### The honest trajectory

| | Vendors | Engine | Revenue |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Year 1** | 15–20 | Hand-sold. Learning. Healthy Habits and the first cohort. | ~£100k |
| **Year 2** | 60–100 | Coach encoded. Self-serve live. Referral working. | ~£700k |
| **Year 3** | 200–400 | Product-led compounding. Optionality opening. | **£2–5m** |

**200 active vendors × ~£20k = £4m.** That is not a side project, and it is reachable — but it is **entirely conditional on engines 1–4 landing in years one and two.**

Which means the build sequence is not a nice-to-have. **It is the plan.** "Ambitious" is not a setting. It is a build.

## 13. What remains existential

The pivot does not solve these, and we should be honest about it.

1. **Throughput — the ceiling that matters.** Two part-time people onboarding one to two vendors a month is not a scaling model, and no amount of TAM fixes it. **This is now the single most important strategic problem we have.** §12.3 sets out the answer, and the encouraging part is that it is a *product* problem rather than a sales one — but until items 1–3 of that sequence are built, our growth rate is capped by Ed's evenings.
2. **The moat is unbuilt.** The two capabilities that constitute our entire differentiation are the two we have built least. **If we spend a year on capture surfaces and never build the moat, we *are* a worse Slerp** — and we will also never break the throughput ceiling, because they are the same build.
3. **Graduation is unproven.** The whole pivot rests on captured vendors eventually running drops. If they don't, we have built a popular free product with a small business attached. **The break-even graduation rate is the number to watch**, and nobody knows it yet.
4. **Retention past drop ten.** Churn, not acquisition, sets the ceiling on the vendor base. Everything in the repetition layer exists to protect this — and productising that coaching is also unlock #1 for throughput.
5. **The funnel is untested.** Healthy Habits was hand-sold, warm, with founder attention. **n=1, and a biased 1.** A successful HH launch proves the product. It proves almost nothing about whether vendor number eight — cold, sceptical, busy — will say yes.
6. **Depth is unproven.** §12.2 assumes a mature vendor can sustain four moments a week across contexts. No vendor has ever done this. It is the most important assumption in the ambition case, and Healthy Habits is where we first test it.
7. **Referral is unproven — and unbuilt.** Engine 4 is the only compounding channel we have, and we currently have **no mechanic for it at all**. If independents don't refer each other, growth stays linear and the year-three number stays small no matter what else we build.
8. **Self-serve is unproven for our model.** Hotplate does zero-touch onboarding, but Hotplate has no host layer, no coaching, and no intelligence. Whether *our* stage one can be genuinely human-free — while preserving the selectivity gate (§10.1) — is an open question. If qualification cannot be self-served, the founder bottleneck survives every other engine.

## Appendix — what does not change

- The drop model and its non-negotiables: pre-order, fixed windows, designed menus, declared capacity respected absolutely.
- Honest scarcity. Real capacity. No manufactured urgency.
- No customer surcharge. No hidden payment margin. No discounting.
- The customer asset belongs to the vendor — exportable, contactable, unconditionally.
- Vendor-first architecture. The vendor is the foreground; Hearth sits quietly behind.
- Selectivity, worn with confidence. Hearth is not for every food business, and saying so builds trust.
- **Hearth grows only when vendors do. There is no other version of success.**
