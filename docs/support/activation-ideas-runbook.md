# Hearth — Vendor Activation Ideas: Manual Onboarding Runbook

**Purpose.** A repeatable process Rob or Ed runs when a new vendor onboards: research
what's known about them, read their archetype, and hand-build a small, curated set of
activation ideas that give them a head start. Delivered off-system at first — a short
note or a conversation — this is the human version of what T5-C6 will later automate.

**Status.** Living document. The method and evidence cards below were derived from three
real runs (Healthy Habits Cafe, Griffs Family Butchers, Lam's Catering) in June 2026.
Each manual run should refine this file. The runs are the training material for the
automated plan; nothing here should be left to imagination once a real vendor exists to
test it against.

**Voice.** Vendor-facing output follows the repetition-layer voice spec and the
banned-word list (no boost / convert / funnel / campaign / promotion / deal / optimise /
leverage / maximise / trending). Honest and restrained — a head start, not a sales pitch.

**Companion file.** The delivered vendor-facing notes this method produces — the worked
outputs for the first three vendors — live alongside this at
`activation-outputs-first-three-vendors.md`.

## The method — eight steps

Each step states what to do, the signals it produces, and a note on what automation can
and can't take over.

### Step 1 — Structured inputs & identity

Pull the platform's own data: archetype, primary goal, delivery model, customer-data
posture, existing host contexts, area, address, social handles, terms.

**Ask directly for what research can't see.** The warmest opportunity is often an existing
demand the vendor already serves badly — a regular order pushed through an aggregator, a
group handled by hand. These leave no online trace; only the vendor knows. Ask plainly:
*what are you currently sending to an aggregator, or handling manually, that you wish you
didn't have to?* Capture the answer as a first-class input — it frequently outranks
anything research turns up (see signal 1).

**Guardrail — confirm identity before anything else.** Small-vendor names collide.
Building ideas off the wrong business poisons every step downstream. Confirm the exact
business via a vendor-supplied URL or handle plus location before researching.

*Automation note:* trivial to pull. The misidentification risk is why the automated
plan must rely on vendor-supplied handles captured at onboarding, not a name lookup.

### Step 2 — Deep research *(the load-bearing step)*

The platform can't see a vendor's real online presence. A person can. This is the step
that most separates a curated plan from a generic one, and the hardest to automate.

**Where to look:**

- **Their website** — what they actually sell, prices, existing products, an online shop,
  any existing pre-order or box format, a loyalty mechanism.
- **Their socials (Instagram, Facebook)** — following size, what they post, the brand
  voice, whether the owner is a visible personality, community ties.
- **Reviews (Google, TripAdvisor, Facebook)** — the *signature dishes and moments
  customers single out by name*. This is the richest ideation seed available.
- **Opening hours (Google Business)** — the days and evenings they're *closed* are free
  capacity a drop can sell into without touching their core trade.
- **Local press, FSA rating, Companies House** — standing, longevity, trust signals,
  founders' names for voice.
- **The matching archetype evidence card** (below) — pull the proven mechanic, the
  occasion calendar, and the comparables for this kind of vendor.

**What to extract:** signature items; existing products / pre-order behaviour; existing
list or loyalty signals; the "dark hours" capacity gap; the occasion calendar that fits
their cuisine and trade; their brand voice; whether they already think in occasions or
cadence.

*Automation note:* the structured half (hours, an online-shop scan, a "do they already
sell boxes" check, social-following size) is automatable. The judgement half — reading
reviews for signature items, sensing brand voice, spotting a community tie — is the last
thing to automate. Each manual run tells us which of these are worth generating at all.

*Blind spot:* research only sees what's public. A vendor's quietly frustrated demand — an
office group-ordering through Just Eat — is invisible here, which is why Step 1's direct
question sits alongside this step, not after it.

### Step 3 — Read / diagnose

Synthesise Steps 1–2 into a one-paragraph read: what they make, who they already reach,
what they're moving away from, where they are. Then resolve the branch signals (next
section) — they determine the opening move.

**Guardrail — every confident claim must trace to something observed.** The delivered note
will say things like "you already run drops at Christmas" or "you leave with one phone
number". When the read is right, that specificity is what makes it land; when it's slightly
wrong, it's worse than generic. Every confident claim must trace to a review, their menu,
or a stated fact — never a guess.

*Automation note:* archetype detection and data posture encode part of this; the
*quality* of the existing audience and the goal-alignment read need a human.

### Step 4 — Opening move (first drop)

Pick the single first drop most likely to fill, keyed to the read. Lowest-risk, warmest
audience, easiest to repeat, capacity set to underpromise. The archetype card gives the
default; the branch signals tell you when to deviate from it.

**Priority rule:** a vendor-stated frustrated demand (signal 1) outranks any inferred
opportunity — it's the warmest, most certain fill there is. Lead with it where it exists;
fall back to the archetype default only when there isn't one.

*Automation note:* the most template-able step — a lookup keyed to archetype plus the
branch signals.

### Step 5 — Audience routing

Who to approach first, and how to reach the people who already value them: their own
socials, their list, a host's group, a counter QR that turns footfall into owned
customers. The reachability principle holds — each channel's owner must be able to reach
that audience.

*Automation note:* host-fit is T5-9 territory; existing-audience activation is the comms
architecture's channel map.

### Step 6 — Ideation

Two to four concrete, vendor-specific drop ideas drawn from the deep research and the
archetype card. This is where a person beats the template. Every idea passes the Hearth
test: reaching people who already value them, honest scarcity, no manufactured urgency.

*Automation note:* the hardest to automate; the manual runs are the pattern library.

### Step 7 — Shape the arc

Sequence the ideas into the first-eight-drops structure (prove the mechanics → repeat
same day → returning customers appear → habit embeds), and state plainly what the
platform handles automatically (list-building, early access, confirmations, thank-yous).
Set honest expectations: early drops may be quiet; cadence is the mechanism.

*Automation note:* close to T5-C6 sections 4–5 — fully deterministic.

### Step 8 — Package, deliver, capture

Deliver off-system first: a short warm note or conversation, in voice, framed as a head
start. **Choose the register from relationship temperature (signal 8):** a warm head start
for a confirmed vendor, a peer opener for a warm prospect, a pitch for a cold approach —
the same method underneath, framed differently. Then capture which ideas they took and how
they landed, into the feedback loop (issue log, and the future pattern library).

*Automation note:* the delivery channel eventually becomes the welcome email / in-app
prompt (deferred scope on T5-C6); the capture step is what makes each run better than the
last.

## The branch signals — what the plan must reason about

The method holds across very different vendors, but the **opening move and the framing
branch hard** on these eight signals. This list is the working spec for the automated plan.
Signals 1 and 8 emerged from the first-three evaluation pass, and matter most for *where to
start* and *how to frame it*.

1. **Vendor-stated frustrated demand.** *(Highest-priority opening-move trigger.)* An
   existing demand the vendor already serves badly — a regular order pushed through an
   aggregator, a group handled by hand. It's the warmest, most certain first fill, and
   usually outranks anything research finds. It's also typically invisible to research, so
   it only surfaces if you ask (Step 1). Healthy Habits' office lunch is the worked case.
2. **Existing host?** Host present → host-first. No host but premises + local audience →
   premises-collection-first. The host need not be a club or school — a **workplace** is a
   private, recurring community node (see Card B). None of the first three vendors had a
   public host; all opened on their own audience or a stated demand.
3. **Existing digital footprint / owned list?** A website shop or loyalty system signals
   import-readiness (use the import path). None of that signals a list-poor vendor whose
   first lever is a counter QR.
4. **Occasion-led or cadence-led demand?** Cadence vendors form a weekly habit; occasion
   vendors spike a few times a year. Most artisans run *both* tracks — a recurring spine
   plus occasion spikes. **Cadence coaching must be track-aware:** never nag an occasion
   vendor for "drops on different days."
5. **Stated goal vs highest-value use.** A vendor's stated goal isn't always where Hearth
   creates most value. Honour the stated goal as the warm entry; name the bigger prize
   alongside it. (See the caterer card.)
6. **Structural capture-leak.** Some archetypes expose their food to many people but
   capture almost none — the owned-asset pitch is sharpest for them. Caterers leak most.
7. **Culture-specific occasion calendar.** The occasion engine must be keyed to the
   vendor's cuisine and trade — Christmas and the World Cup for a butcher; Chinese New
   Year and Mid-Autumn for a Cantonese caterer.
8. **Relationship temperature.** *(Selects register, not content.)* Confirmed vendor → warm
   head start; warm prospect → peer opener; cold approach → pitch. The same method
   underneath, framed differently (Step 8).

## Archetype evidence cards

Each card is reusable in vendor conversations and is a credibility source the automated
plan can eventually cite. Data points are attributed; see Sources.

### Card A — Traditional / whole-carcass butcher
*Worked example: Griffs Family Butchers, Broadstone*

**Signature of the archetype.** Shop with footfall and a loyal local base; often an
online shop and a loyalty card (so list-warmer than most); demand is occasion-led but
with a natural weekly anchor available; meat from whole carcasses means the whole animal
must sell, not just prime cuts.

**The structural read.** Two tracks. A *recurring spine* (weekly meat box, or a "Saturday
collection for your Sunday roast" box using the closed-Sunday capacity) builds the owned
list and forms the habit. *Occasion spikes* (BBQ, sporting events, Christmas) are fired at
the list the spine has built. The spine is not optional: for a whole-carcass butcher, a
box is how you *balance the carcass* — selling all the parts, not only the popular cuts,
which is cited as a reason small butchers fail (Primal Meats).

**Proven mechanic + data.**
- The meat-subscription model is established and growing — roughly $2bn globally in 2025,
  growing ~14% a year (Persistence Market Research); ButcherBox built a large DTC business
  on exactly this.
- UK comparables on the doorstep: the Dorset Meat Company (same county) was voted Best
  Meat Boxes by the Independent.
- **Christmas proves the drop model with the butcher's own peak.** Independent butchers
  already run capacity-limited, pre-paid, time-slotted Christmas pre-orders that sell out
  and close their order books — one Welsh butcher had ~95% of stock pre-ordered well ahead
  of Christmas, with outlets closing their goose books on demand (North Wales Live); others
  run a separate festive shop with date-and-time collection slots and limited spaces per
  day (Saunderson's; Reed's). That *is* a drop. The pitch writes itself: you already run
  drops at Christmas — Hearth lets you run them all year and keep the customers.
- **Occasion data (sporting / BBQ):** the 2026 World Cup is forecast to drive ~£3.8bn of
  UK consumer spending, food and drink the top category at ~£1.95bn, with 31.2m fans
  watching from home (VoucherCodes via Talking Retail / Asian Trader). The AHDB notes
  summer tournaments lift BBQ cuts specifically — burgers, grills, marinades, ribs — at
  the expense of roasting joints and mince. (Realism: tournament uplift is real but
  fleeting and skews to beer — BRC; treat an occasion box as an acquisition spike, not a
  sustainable line.)

**Opening-move pattern.** Lead the strategic framing with the recurring box (balance-the-
carcass economics). Use the nearest big occasion (a summer sporting / BBQ box) as the
*launch* drop — easy win, seeds the list fast — then roll those buyers into the recurring
spine. Honest scarcity is often literally true here (Christmas birds, whole-carcass cuts).

**Occasion calendar.** Christmas (peak), Easter, summer BBQ season, bank holidays, major
sporting fixtures.

**Model-fit caveat.** Strong fit. Capacity caps are genuine physical limits, which makes
the honest-scarcity story the easiest of any archetype.

### Card B — Independent health café moving off aggregators
*Worked example: Healthy Habits Cafe, Broadstone*

**Signature of the archetype.** Strong daily footfall and a loyal, often health-conscious
base; thousands of social followers; high ratings; on Deliveroo / Just Eat and unhappy
with it; frequently *list-poor but audience-rich* — large reachable audience, but no owned
list because the aggregators keep it. Usually closed evenings and/or a day a week. Often
carries a quiet B2B demand too — a local office or team wanting a group lunch — that the
aggregator serves badly.

**The structural read.** The owned list is the missing asset, and the dark hours
(evenings, closed days) are the capacity to sell into. A recurring drop into a slot
they're currently closed builds the list without touching their core trade.

**The workplace lunch — often the strongest opening move.** Where the café already has an
office or team group-ordering through an aggregator, that's the warmest first drop
available (signal 1), and usually beats any box we'd infer. Model it as a **private,
recurring, community-scoped Hosted drop**: the office is a community node (no revenue
share), `audience_scope = community` so it's not public, individual payment, each person
ordering their own personalised lunch via one link by a set cut-off. The shipped dietary
flags do real work here — a curated healthy menu, each person picking their own, allergens
visible. It's a near-certain weekly fill, a pre-built work routine (strong cadence), and a
direct, concrete displacement of the aggregator habit. It is also invisible to research —
it surfaces only by asking. **Model-fit caveats to confirm, not overpromise:** individual
payment fits today; a single consolidated company invoice is a gap (Stripe Connect is
per-order); a true hands-off standing order ("the usual", auto each week) is post-launch —
V1 is each person ordering within the window, still far calmer than the aggregator.

**Proven mechanic + data.**
- **Why leave the aggregators, current and quantified:** Deliveroo / Uber Eats run ~25–35%
  commission plus fees (Deliverect); operators report it working out higher — one London
  café owner put Deliveroo's effective take at ~42% per order with VAT (CNBC). A 2026 UK
  guide frames the marketplaces as ~25–35% with *no customer capture*, ~£12 per order at a
  £40 basket (MealShift). The market is consolidating, not softening: DoorDash acquired
  Deliveroo for ~£2.9bn, completing late 2025 (Deliverect/CB Insights).
- **The recurring healthy box has a market behind it:** UK food-subscription-box demand
  was ~$6.2bn in 2025 with the UK leading the category on growth (~11% CAGR), driven by
  predictable, recurring purchase cycles (FactMR). The UK meal-replacement market
  (~$1.3bn, health-conscious, plant-based-led) is growing steadily (Future Market
  Insights). HH already has the product credibility and local trust; the cadence demand is
  proven.

**Opening-move pattern.** If a vendor-stated demand exists (an office lunch), lead with it
— it's the warmest, most certain fill. Otherwise premises-collection-first (no host
needed): a recurring box into a closed slot — a "Sunday reset" week-ahead box, or a
Friday-evening healthy takeaway — to their warm local audience. Either way, the
highest-leverage list-building lever is a **counter QR** turning daily footfall into the
owned list. A health-affinity host (gym, yoga, wellness studio) is the natural drop 3–4
expansion.

**Occasion calendar.** New Year / January reset (genuine health-café spike), summer,
seasonal resets.

**Model-fit caveat.** Strong fit. Watch the list-poor/audience-rich distinction: the
customer-data-posture field captures exportable-list richness, not real-world reach — the
plan needs both dimensions.

### Card C — Occasion / event caterer (no storefront)
*Worked example: Lam's Catering (Cantonese dim sum), Dorset*

**Signature of the archetype.** A kitchen without a permanent retail front: private /
in-home or event catering, single-payer per job, headcount minimums, occasion-led
(birthdays, banquets, holidays). Often already productised into per-head packages. Lumpy,
volatile, economic-confidence-sensitive demand (UK catering ~£1.4bn, fluctuating —
IBISWorld).

**The structural read — the sharpest capture-leak of any archetype.** Every job exposes
the food to 10–40 guests who love it, and the caterer captures only the host. The people
who just discovered they love the food are never reachable again. High commitment
minimums (a headcount and several hundred pounds) also bar everyone who'd love the food
but has no occasion big enough. Both problems have the same fix: an owned audience built
through low-commitment individual-pay drops.

**The positioning move — honour the stated goal, name the prize.** These vendors often ask
for *event orchestration*. Hearth can do that (single-payer Event drops — private link,
fixed package, fixed date), and it fits a productised caterer well. But the transformation
is the *customer-engagement* layer: neighbourhood / individual-pay drops that (a) remove
the minimum-commitment barrier so anyone can try the food, (b) capture every taster as a
named customer, and (c) feed more event enquiries back. It's a loop — events expose the
food → drops capture and retain the tasters → the owned audience generates more events.
Lead with their ask as the warm entry; anchor the pitch on "stop losing everyone who
tastes your food."

**Proven mechanic + data.**
- **The DTC escape is documented:** caterers pivoted to meal kits, boxes and reheat-at-home
  formats (Deloitte); the UK meal-kit sector has grown ~fourfold since 2018, on the
  explicit logic that customers who tried you once can now order from home (Chefs'
  Insider / The Wholesale Group). Occasion boxes for at-home gatherings are a live 2026
  trend — sports watch-party boxes, event "at-home" boxes (CFE News). But small caterers
  lack the tech to run pre-ordered DTC at scale (Coherent Market Insights) — Hearth is that
  missing layer.
- **Hotplate is the direct proof-of-model for this archetype.** Hotplate (US, $50M+ in
  sales) serves exactly the storefront-less category — pop-ups, chefs, supper clubs — and
  is explicit that "most pop-ups already sell this way; Hotplate just gives it structure."
  It solves both halves of the caterer's problem: *existing customers* via an owned SMS /
  email list (the bigger the list, the more immediate the sales when a drop opens; lists
  can be imported wholesale) and the ability to "see who my repeats are"; *new customers*
  via the line from social content / going viral into local orders, plus sold-out
  reputation. That is precisely what a caterer lacks today — every event a cold start, no
  owned list, no view of repeats. (Hotplate caveats: US, baker/pop-up-skewed, and it leans
  on countdown/urgency mechanics Hearth deliberately rejects — Hearth's scarcity is real,
  and the host/community-node layer is the differentiator.)

**Opening-move pattern.** A low-commitment neighbourhood box (for Lam's: a weekend "at
home" dim sum box, individual pre-order, collection / local delivery) to their existing
following — the wedge that removes the minimum-commitment barrier and seeds the owned list.
Layer Event drops on top for their existing bookings so they feel heard. A "banquet in a
box" bridges box-buyer to event-client.

**Occasion calendar (culture-specific).** Chinese New Year (the marquee peak — the
Cantonese equivalent of the butcher's Christmas), Mid-Autumn / mooncakes. For other
caterers, key to their cuisine and the events they already serve.

**Model-fit caveat.** Hearth handles productised single-payer events well, but **not**
bespoke quoting / negotiation / invoicing (T5-22 deferred). Productised caterers (fixed
per-head packages) fit; heavily bespoke caterers only partly. Flag this honestly rather
than overpromising. New operational shift to name: neighbourhood drops mean batch-cooking
for collection, not cooking in situ — a genuine new product line (dim sum travels and
reheats well, so it suits).

## Cross-cutting evidence — the drop model itself

Worth keeping to hand for any archetype. **Hotplate** is the closest direct comparable to
Hearth and validates the model: $50M+ in sales across the US on pre-order drops, with
three lessons that mirror Hearth's locked strategy almost exactly — consistent scheduled
drops grow faster and produce more repeat customers because customers form a habit around
the schedule (the 8–10-drop thesis); fewer options sell more, built around one hero
product (the curated-menu principle); each vendor has their own URL with no competing
vendors shown, advertising through their own socials (vendor-first, no aggregation). It
works best once a vendor has even a small list to turn into momentum.

**The deliberate divergence:** Hotplate leans on countdown timers and an urgency loop.
Hearth rejects manufactured urgency — its scarcity is real, and the host/community-node
and local-economy-graph layers are what make it more than a UK Hotplate. **Caveat:** US,
baker/pop-up-skewed — treat as proof-of-model, not a like-for-like UK benchmark.
