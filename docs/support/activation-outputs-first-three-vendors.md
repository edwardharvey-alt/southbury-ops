# Activation outputs — first three vendors (draft for evaluation)

These are the **delivered form** of the method: what the vendor actually receives or hears,
built from the internal read but stripped of the evidence and data (those stay behind the
note). Three vendors, three registers — worth deciding whether that difference is right.

- **Healthy Habits Cafe** — a warm head start for a confirmed first vendor; shared inside
  the onboarding conversation.
- **Griffs Family Butchers** — a head start that doubles as the opener; peer-to-peer, since
  a butcher already runs sell-out Christmas pre-orders.
- **Lam's Catering** — a pitch, not an onboarding note; same method, aimed at winning
  interest.

---

## 1 — Healthy Habits Cafe
*For Molly and Brennan. Use: shared in the onboarding conversation as a warm starting
point. Leads with the vendor-stated office-lunch demand — the warmest first drop available.*

Hi Molly and Brennan,

A few thoughts on where we'd start — shaped around what you've already built, and the one
thing you've told us isn't working.

**Where we'd start — the office lunch.** You've got a local office trying to order their
lunch from you as a group, and Just Eat makes a mess of it. This is the clearest first drop
you have. We'd set it up as a private, recurring lunchtime order just for them: you choose
the menu and the day, they each order their own lunch through a single link by a set
cut-off, and you make it in one calm batch. Personalised for each person, controlled by
you, and none of the aggregator's chaos or its cut. It's a near-certain fill every week,
and the warmest possible place to begin.

Then, alongside it:

**The Sunday reset box** — order through the week, collect Sunday morning. It uses a day
you're closed and reaches your wider following, building a list that's yours.

A couple more, once you've found your rhythm:

- A January drop, when the appetite for eating well is highest.
- A drop hosted at a local gym or studio for their members.

**What to expect.** The office lunch should fill from the start — it's a routine people
already want. The wider drops take longer; the first few may be quieter than you'd hope,
and holding the same day each week is what turns them into something people plan around.
From day one, a small QR code by the till turns the people who already walk in into a list
that's yours — something the delivery apps never let you keep. Hearth handles the rest:
early access, confirmations, and a thank-you the morning after with the next date.

The office order is the obvious place to prove it. Everything else builds from there.

## 2 — Griffs Family Butchers
*For Sean. Use: opens the conversation — a butcher who already runs sell-out Christmas
pre-orders is half-way to the model already.*

Hi Sean,

A few thoughts on where Griffs could start — built around what you already do well.

What we see: a butcher with a real reputation, an online shop, a loyalty card, and meat
boxes already on the go. You're closer to this than most — and every Christmas, when you
take pre-orders and your books fill up and close, you're already running exactly what we're
describing. The idea is simply to run that all year, and to keep the customers it brings
you.

**Where we'd start.** With the World Cup on, a match-weekend BBQ box is an easy first drop
— a set number of boxes, pre-ordered, collected on the Saturday. It's timely, it's the kind
of thing people share, and it brings new names in quickly.

A few drops we think would suit you:

- The World Cup BBQ box — your opening drop, while the tournament's on.
- A weekly Sunday roast box: ordered through the week, collected Saturday. It uses a day
  you're closed and helps balance the whole carcass, not just the prime cuts.
- Christmas, run through Hearth — the drop you already prove every year, except this time
  the customers stay yours afterwards.

**What to expect.** The recurring roast box is the quiet engine — it's what turns one-off
buyers into regulars. You can bring your existing customers in from the start, so you're
not beginning cold. Honest capacity is on your side here: when the boxes for a slot are
gone, the drop closes — no overpromising. Hearth looks after early access, confirmations,
and the morning-after thank-you with the next date.

Worth a conversation whenever suits.

## 3 — Lam's Catering
*For Christine. Use: a pitch, not an onboarding note — built from the same method, but
aimed at winning interest.*

Hi Christine,

A thought about Lam's, after looking at what you've built since 2007.

Every event you cater, a room full of people taste your food and love it — and at the end
of the night, you leave with one phone number: the person who booked you. Twenty, thirty,
forty people have just discovered they love your dim sum, and there's no way to reach any
of them again. And because your packages start at a table's worth of guests, plenty of
people who'd love your food never get the occasion to try it at all.

Both of those are the same missing piece: a way to turn the people who taste your food into
customers you can reach directly. That's what Hearth is for.

**Where we'd start.** A weekend "Lam's at home" dim sum box — people order ahead, then
collect or have it dropped locally. Low commitment, no minimum table, just your food at
someone's kitchen table on a Friday night. It's new income, and every person who orders
becomes a name you keep.

A few ways it could grow:

- The weekend dim sum box — the easiest place to begin.
- A banquet-in-a-box for smaller gatherings — your full spread, without the minimum or the
  in-home service.
- A Chinese New Year drop — your busiest moment of the year, run properly.
- And your existing event packages, taken and managed through Hearth, so bookings are
  tidier too.

The two sides feed each other — the people who try a box become your next event enquiry,
and the guests at an event become box customers. Over time you're not starting cold with
each booking; you're building an audience that's yours.

**Honest notes.** Hearth handles your set packages and these drops well — it isn't built
for fully bespoke quoting. And a box means cooking for collection rather than in someone's
home — a new line, though dim sum travels beautifully.

If it's of interest, the first box is a low-risk way to see how it feels.

## Evaluation prompts (decide before documenting)

- **Evidence level:** the delivered note keeps only the warm read + a few ideas; all market
  data sits behind it. Right call, or should a line or two of proof appear (e.g. to Sean,
  "butchers up and down the country already sell out Christmas pre-orders this way")?
- **Register:** three different registers (head start / peer opener / pitch). Keep the
  difference, or standardise one house style?
- **The "what we see" opening:** demonstrates attention and builds warmth — but pressure-
  test whether being visibly researched feels good or slightly studied, especially cold
  (Lam's).
- **Scope:** ~300 words, 3–4 ideas — a head start, not a full plan. Right size?
- **Delivery:** written note vs conversation scaffold. These read as notes; the dry run may
  show a conversation lands better, with the note left behind.
